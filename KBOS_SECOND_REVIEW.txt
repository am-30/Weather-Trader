A. EVALUATION OF GEMINI AUDIT FINDINGS
================================================================================

I'll evaluate each of Gemini's five findings for validity and whether the

FINDING 1: Attractor Hyper-Inflation via Double Counting
----------------------------------------------------------
Gemini's diagnosis: VALID and CRITICAL.
Gemini's proposed fix: PARTIALLY VALID but architecturally over-complicated.

The diagnosis is correct. Looking at the March 29 worked example:
  kalman_bias = +2.02°F
  morning_drift_adjustment = +1.151°F
  nwp_anchor_offset = +2.06°F
  Total shift above NWP: +5.23°F

The blended NWP peak is 49.1°F. The effective attractor peak is 54.3°F. For
a market with strikes around 48-52°F, this pushes the entire probability mass
to the right, explaining why the model assigns P(YES) = 100% to the 48.5
strike — the distribution's LEFT TAIL doesn't even reach 48.5°F when the
attractor center is at 54°F.

This is the single biggest accuracy problem in the current model.

HOWEVER: Gemini's proposed fix (restructure the Kalman filter so its
measurement innovation subtracts drift_adj from the NWP reference) is
architecturally invasive and introduces a different problem. If you define:
  innovation = ASOS - (NWP + drift_adj)
then the Kalman bias state B_t is estimating TODAY'S residual beyond the
historical average error. This is conceptually elegant but:
  a) It couples drift calibration to Kalman behavior — any change in drift
     calibration retroactively changes the meaning of B_t.
  b) With only 8 settled dates, drift_adj is itself noisy (+1.15°F has wide
     confidence intervals). Baking a noisy estimate into the Kalman reference
     means the filter is correcting for noise in the drift estimate, not
     genuine intraday signal.

MY RECOMMENDED FIX (simpler, more robust):
  The drift_adj and kalman_bias are fundamentally trying to correct the same
  thing: NWP systematic error. They should NOT both be in the attractor.
  The correct approach is:

  Option 1 (RECOMMENDED): Remove drift_adj from the attractor entirely.
    mu_t = nwp_curve[h] + nwp_anchor_offset + kalman_bias
    The Kalman bias B_t already absorbs the systematic NWP error in real-time.
    The drift adjustment was needed when B_t was stuck at zero (old H=[[1,0]]).
    With the fixed H=[[1,1]], B_t converges in hours and should subsume what
    drift_adj was doing.

    Keep calibrating drift_adj in the background as a DIAGNOSTIC — compare it
    to kalman_bias to verify they're tracking the same signal. If they diverge
    significantly, that's a sign of a problem in the Kalman filter.

  Option 2 (CONSERVATIVE): Use drift_adj as a FLOOR on kalman_bias.
    effective_bias = max(kalman_bias, drift_adj) if both positive
    mu_t = nwp_curve[h] + nwp_anchor_offset + effective_bias
    This prevents the attractor from ignoring persistent NWP bias on the first
    few hours of a new day before the Kalman filter has converged.

  Option 1 is simpler and should be tried first. If backtesting shows that
  the first 2-3 hours of the day (before B_t converges) have degraded accuracy
  without drift_adj, switch to Option 2.


FINDING 2: Second-Order Kinematic Filter & Covariance Explosion
-----------------------------------------------------------------
Gemini's diagnosis of covariance explosion: VALID.
Gemini's proposed fix (second-order filter): OVERKILL and risky.

The covariance values of P ≈ [[40, -40], [-40, 40]] are genuinely pathological.
With Q_temp=0.1 and R=0.4, a converged 2D Kalman filter should have P[0,0]
in the range 0.1-0.5 after 50+ ASOS updates. P=40 means the filter has either:
  a) Run many predict steps without updates (gap inflation gone wrong), or
  b) Has a structural issue in how P is persisted/restored across sessions.

The near-perfect anti-correlation (P[0,1] ≈ -P[0,0]) is also a red flag. It
means the filter believes that ANY positive shock to the departure state implies
an equally large negative shock to the bias state. This is physically wrong —
a warm ASOS reading should update BOTH dT and B upward, not push them in
opposite directions.

HOWEVER: Gemini's proposed second-order filter (tracking temperature velocity)
is solving a problem that doesn't exist. The OU Monte Carlo already models
the temperature trajectory — the Kalman filter's job is just to estimate the
current departure and bias, not to predict the trajectory. Adding a velocity
state doubles the state dimension, requires calibrating new Q entries, and
introduces complexity with no clear benefit for a system that runs MC every
5 minutes.

The innovation gate (Correction 2.3) is a good idea but should be simpler:
just clamp innovations, don't fractionally update.

MY RECOMMENDED FIXES:

  Fix 2A — Covariance Cap:
    After every update step, enforce:
      P[0,0] = min(P[0,0], P_MAX_DIAG)  where P_MAX_DIAG = 2.0
      P[1,1] = min(P[1,1], P_MAX_DIAG)
      # Enforce positive semi-definiteness after capping:
      max_offdiag = sqrt(P[0,0] * P[1,1])
      P[0,1] = clip(P[0,1], -max_offdiag * 0.8, max_offdiag * 0.8)
      P[1,0] = P[0,1]

    The 0.8 factor on off-diagonal prevents near-singular covariance matrices.
    P_MAX_DIAG = 2.0 means the filter's uncertainty in the departure state
    is at most sqrt(2) ≈ 1.4°F, which is reasonable for a few hours of
    uncertainty growth.

  Fix 2B — Innovation Gate:
    Before the Kalman update step:
      S = H @ P @ H.T + R  (innovation covariance)
      innovation = z - H @ x
      mahalanobis = abs(innovation) / sqrt(S)
      if mahalanobis > 4.0:
          log warning "ASOS reading rejected: {innovation}°F innovation, {mahalanobis} sigma"
          skip this update entirely (do not update x or P)

    Threshold of 4.0 sigma (not 3.0 as Gemini suggests) because ASOS readings
    can legitimately jump 1.8°F (one 0.5°C persistence step at a time), and
    with P collapsed near 0.1, a 1.8°F innovation could be 5+ sigma even
    though it's physically valid. 4.0 sigma with P capped at 2.0 means we
    reject only innovations > ~6°F, which would be genuine data corruption.

  Fix 2C — Gap Inflation Cap:
    The current gap inflation runs up to 12 predict steps with no updates.
    Each predict step adds Q to P: P += [[0.1, 0], [0, 0.05]].
    After 12 steps: P[0,0] += 1.2, P[1,1] += 0.6.
    Starting from a converged P ≈ 0.3, this gives P[0,0] ≈ 1.5. Fine.

    But if gap inflation runs on TOP of a warm-start with 1.2x inflation
    from yesterday's already-large P, it compounds. Verify that gap inflation
    checks P AFTER the warm-start inflation, and caps the result.

    Specific fix: After all initialization (warm-start + gap inflation),
    apply the covariance cap from Fix 2A. This guarantees P starts the day
    in a reasonable range regardless of yesterday's state.


FINDING 3: Remove Anchor Offset Entirely
-------------------------------------------
Gemini's diagnosis: PARTIALLY VALID.
Gemini's proposed fix: TOO AGGRESSIVE without backtesting.

Gemini is right that the anchor offset can interfere with OU mean-reversion
mechanics. When the anchor offset raises mu_t to match T0, the OU process
has nothing to revert TO — the "pull" disappears and paths diffuse freely.

BUT: Gemini's claim that this "fundamentally breaks" OU mechanics is overstated.
The anchor offset only affects the STARTING gap. As the simulation progresses,
mu_t tracks the NWP curve (which rises and falls with the diurnal cycle), and
the OU process reverts toward it normally. The anchor offset is a one-time
initialization correction, not a per-step override.

The real issue with the anchor offset (from the original review) is the
double-counting with Kalman bias. The current code already subtracts
kalman_bias from the raw gap:
  gap_after_bias = raw_gap - kalman_bias
  nwp_anchor_offset = gap_after_bias * anchor_weight

This is correct in principle. The remaining concern is whether anchor_weight
itself is well-calibrated.

MY RECOMMENDATION:
  Do NOT remove the anchor offset yet. Instead:
  1. Fix the attractor inflation (Finding 1 — remove drift_adj) first.
  2. Run the backtester with and without the anchor offset.
  3. If the backtester shows no improvement from the offset, remove it.
  4. If it helps in the morning but hurts in the afternoon, consider making
     anchor_weight = 0 for hours past the NWP peak.

  The anchor offset is a reasonable heuristic for a system with only 8 days
  of calibration data. As the Kalman bias converges over weeks, the offset
  will naturally shrink (gap_after_bias → 0 as B_t absorbs systematic error).


FINDING 4: Lookahead Bias in Model Calibration
-------------------------------------------------
Gemini's diagnosis: PARTIALLY VALID but overstated.
Gemini's proposed fix: IMPRACTICAL with free Open-Meteo data.

Gemini correctly notes that HRRR updates hourly with assimilated data while
ECMWF uses older base runs, so comparing 10 AM HRRR against 10 AM ECMWF
isn't truly apples-to-apples. The HRRR 10 AM run has "seen" the 9 AM
temperature; the ECMWF 10 AM run was produced from the 00Z or 06Z base run.

HOWEVER: Gemini's fix (use only 12Z runs) is impractical because Open-Meteo's
free API doesn't provide model-run-specific forecasts. You get the "best
available" forecast at fetch time, which is a blend of recent model runs.
Requesting 12Z-specific runs would require paid API access or scraping NOMADS
directly (complex, brittle, not recommended at this stage).

The current approach (first fetch in [10 AM, 1 PM) window) is a reasonable
compromise. The real concern is minor: HRRR gets a slight advantage because
its 10 AM fetch incorporates more recent observations. With only 3 models
and weights near 1/3 each, this bias has minimal practical impact on the
blended forecast.

MY RECOMMENDATION:
  No change needed. The current [10 AM, 1 PM) morning window is adequate.
  Monitor whether HRRR weight drifts significantly above GFS/ECMWF over the
  next 30 days. If it does (HRRR > 0.5), consider shifting the morning window
  to [8 AM, 10 AM) to reduce HRRR's assimilation advantage.


FINDING 5: Probabilistic Floor Injection
-------------------------------------------
Gemini's diagnosis: VALID — the static 0.30°F offset is wrong.
Gemini's proposed fix: CONCEPTUALLY INTERESTING but wrong in detail.

Gemini proposes replacing the static offset with:
  paths_max = current_max_observed + np.random.uniform(0.0, 0.9, n_paths)

This is trying to model the sensor quantization uncertainty, which is a real
physical effect. But uniform(0.0, 0.9) is wrong for several reasons:
  a) The gap isn't uniform. From the empirical data, 6 of 8 dates show a
     +1°F gap and 2 show 0°F. This is a bimodal distribution, not uniform.
  b) 0.9°F is the Celsius step size, but the actual gap depends on WHERE
     the true temperature falls relative to the nearest Celsius threshold.
     If the true max is 39.4°F (4.11°C), it's 0.4°F above the 39.2°F
     (4.0°C) sensor step. The gap is position-dependent.
  c) The uniform approach injects noise that persists through the entire
     simulation — each path starts at a different effective floor and that
     difference never disappears. This artificially widens the distribution
     at the bottom.

The REAL problem is simpler and more urgent: the current offset is 0.30°F
but the empirical data shows a mean gap of 0.75°F (or 1.0°F if you only
count positive gaps). The offset clamp of [0.0, 0.5] is preventing the
calibrated value from reaching the empirically correct level.

MY RECOMMENDED FIX (Phase A, see below):
  1. Raise the persistence_filter_offset clamp to [0.0, 1.5]°F.
  2. Re-run the calibration — with 6/8 dates showing +1°F gaps, the
     calibrated value will land near 0.75-1.0°F.
  3. Keep the static offset approach for now. It's simple and the empirical
     evidence strongly supports a value near 0.75°F.
  4. LATER (Phase B): Consider Gemini's probabilistic approach but with a
     better distribution model. Instead of uniform(0, 0.9), use an empirical
     distribution fitted to the observed ASOS-to-NWS gaps. With 8 data points
     this is premature — wait for 30+ settled dates.

B. NEW ISSUES IDENTIFIED FROM THE TECHNICAL DESCRIPTION

These are problems I found in the technical description that Gemini did not

ISSUE B1: THE DRIFT AND BIAS ARE CORRELATED BUT TREATED AS INDEPENDENT
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Severity: HIGH
Related to: Gemini Finding 1, but a deeper structural point.

Looking at the calibration history:
  March 26: drift_AM = -0.03, drift_PM = +0.11 (NWP was accurate)
  March 29: drift_AM = +1.15, drift_PM = +1.13 (NWP underpredicting)
  March 29: kalman_bias = +2.02 (NWP underpredicting)

Both drift and bias respond to the same underlying phenomenon (NWP systematic
error), but on different timescales:
  - drift_adj is a 14-day rolling average of MC prediction errors
  - kalman_bias is an intraday real-time estimate from ASOS observations

When the NWP is systematically wrong (as it appears to be for March 27-29),
BOTH corrections grow simultaneously. They are NOT independent corrections —
they're correlated estimates of the same thing. Adding them is like averaging
a measurement twice and adding both averages.

The worked example shows the consequence: the attractor peak reaches 54.3°F
(NWP peak 49.1 + bias 2.02 + drift 1.15 + anchor 2.06), which is 5°F above
the raw NWP. The actual high for March 29 will likely be in the 48-52°F range
based on the NWP consensus. A 5°F correction is implausible.

This is the ROOT CAUSE of the 100% fair value probabilities in the snapshots.

Fix: See Phase A, Item 1 below.


ISSUE B2: SIGMA VALUES ARE TOO HIGH (LIKELY STILL CONTAMINATED BY TREND)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------
Severity: HIGH

The current sigma values are:
  Block 6-10:  1.41°F/√hr
  Block 10-14: 1.23°F/√hr
  Block 14-18: 1.14°F/√hr

These imply per-step (5-minute) noise of:
  1.41 * sqrt(5/60) = 0.41°F per 5-minute step

And stationary standard deviations (sigma / sqrt(2*theta)):
  Block 6-10: 1.41 / sqrt(2*0.2896) = 1.85°F
  Block 10-14: 1.23 / sqrt(2*0.2896) = 1.62°F

A stationary std of 1.85°F means the OU process can routinely wander 3.7°F
(2 sigma) from the attractor. Combined with the 5°F attractor inflation from
Issue B1, paths can reach 58°F on a day with a 49°F NWP peak.

The sigma values themselves may be inflated. The calibration uses hourly diffs
detrended by NWP:
  dT_residual = (ASOS[h+1] - ASOS[h]) - (NWP[h+1] - NWP[h])

But the NWP detrending assumes the NWP hourly shape is accurate even if the
level is wrong. If the NWP shape is also wrong (e.g., NWP predicts 2°F/hr
warming at 9 AM but ASOS shows 3°F/hr because of faster-than-expected
clearing), the residual includes shape error, not just random volatility.
This inflates sigma.

Evidence from the parameter history: sigma dropped from 2.0 to 0.97 between
March 15 and March 23, then stabilized at 1.0-1.2. The early 2.0 values were
clearly wrong (insufficient data). The current 1.0-1.2 range is more
reasonable but may still be 20-30% high due to NWP shape error contamination.

Fix: This is hard to fix without more data. For now, the sigma cap
(ou_max_stationary_std) is the correct guardrail. But the cap is currently
at 2.0°F (default), which is too loose. The empirical NWP RMSE of 2.0°F
(blended, from 8 dates) suggests the cap should be closer to 1.5°F.
See Phase A, Item 3.


ISSUE B3: THE DRIFT CALIBRATION FORMULA IS INVERTED
------------------------------------------------------
Severity: HIGH (if confirmed — needs code verification)

The technical description says (Section 7.6):
  morning_drift = mean(final_official_high - predicted_mean_max when ET_hour < 12)

If this is the actual formula in the code, the sign and interpretation are:
  - Positive drift means the model was UNDERPREDICTING (official > predicted)
  - The drift is ADDED to mu_t: mu_t = NWP + anchor + bias + drift_adj

This means: when the model underpredicts, drift_adj is positive, which raises
the attractor, which should improve accuracy. This is correct in direction.

BUT: The drift is computed from MC snapshots that ALREADY include kalman_bias.
So the predicted_mean_max used in the drift calibration already incorporates
whatever bias correction was active on the historical date. If the Kalman bias
was underestimating the true NWP error on those dates (plausible, since the
filter was using H=[[1,0]] until recently), then drift_adj captured the
RESIDUAL error — the part that kalman_bias missed.

Now that kalman_bias works correctly (H=[[1,1]]), it should absorb most of
the systematic error, and drift_adj should shrink toward zero. But the
historical dates used for drift calibration (March 15-28) mostly had the OLD
broken Kalman filter. So drift_adj is calibrated to compensate for the broken
filter, and now the fixed filter is ALSO compensating. Double correction.

This is another manifestation of Issue B1, but it explains WHY the drift
is still large (+1.15°F) even though the Kalman bias is now working correctly:
the drift was calibrated from dates when the bias was broken.

Fix: Once drift_adj is removed from the attractor (Phase A, Item 1), this
issue resolves itself. The drift should still be calibrated and monitored
as a diagnostic, but it should NOT be added to mu_t while kalman_bias is
functioning correctly.

If you want to keep drift_adj as a safety net for the first few hours of
each day (before the Kalman bias converges), compute it ONLY from dates where
the Kalman bias was functioning (H=[[1,1]], i.e. March 27 onward). With only
2-3 qualifying dates, the drift estimate will be noisy and should be heavily
damped (e.g., multiply by 0.3 before adding to mu_t).


ISSUE B4: THETA COMPUTATION METHOD SHOULD USE ln(phi), NOT (1-phi)/dt
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Severity: MEDIUM

The technical description shows (Section 7.3):
  Y = phi * X + epsilon
  theta = (1 - phi) / dt

This is the discrete-time approximation. The exact conversion is:
  theta = -ln(phi) / dt

For small theta*dt (which is the case here: theta ≈ 0.3, dt ≈ 5/60 = 0.083,
so theta*dt ≈ 0.025), both formulas give similar results. But for the hourly
calibration (dt = 1 hour), the difference matters:
  phi = 0.75 → theta_approx = (1-0.75)/1 = 0.25/hr
  phi = 0.75 → theta_exact = -ln(0.75)/1 = 0.288/hr

The 15% discrepancy systematically underestimates theta, which means the OU
process mean-reverts too slowly, paths wander further from the attractor,
and tail probabilities are inflated.

Looking at the current values:
  theta_am = 0.2896 (if this was computed with -ln(phi), then phi ≈ 0.75)
  If it was computed with (1-phi)/dt, the "true" theta should be ~0.34.

Fix: Verify which formula is in the code. If it's (1-phi)/dt, change to
-ln(phi)/dt. This is a one-line fix with a meaningful impact on the
stationary distribution width.


ISSUE B5: THE 100% FAIR VALUE PROBABILITY IS LIKELY A DISPLAY/STORAGE BUG
---------------------------------------------------------------------------
Severity: MEDIUM-HIGH (if it affects trading decisions)

The technical description flags this (Section 8, item 7) and Appendix C
notes it as suspicious. Several possibilities:

  a) The snapshot is storing P(max >= hard_floor) instead of P(max >= strike).
     Since hard_floor is always <= paths_max by construction, this is always 1.0.

  b) The snapshot is storing the probability for the WRONG bucket. If it's
     computing P(YES) for the bottom bucket (which includes all outcomes
     below the strike), and the model assigns ~0% to the bottom bucket,
     then 1 - P(bottom) = 1.0 for the complementary event.

  c) The model genuinely assigns P=100% because the attractor is so inflated
     (Issue B1) that no path falls below the strike.

Possibility (c) is the most concerning because it means the edge table is
also showing inflated probabilities, not just the snapshots. Possibility (a)
would mean the edge table is correct but the snapshots are misleading.

Fix: This needs code inspection. Check what value is stored in
model_fair_value_prob in the snapshot code path. Trace the variable from
the MC output to the database insert. If it's a storage bug, fix the storage.
If the MC output itself is 1.0, that confirms the attractor inflation issue.


ISSUE B6: DRIFT_ADJ IS APPLIED AS A CONSTANT FOR THE ENTIRE SIMULATION
-------------------------------------------------------------------------
Severity: MEDIUM

From Appendix C:
  "drift_adj is fixed at the time MC is constructed (morning or afternoon
   based on current ET hour), NOT re-selected step by step. At 11:52 AM
   (ET hour 10), drift_adj = morning_drift = +1.151°F for all 168 steps."

This means a simulation starting at 10 AM applies the MORNING drift to all
168 steps, including the afternoon steps (hours 12-23). But the morning and
afternoon drifts can differ — the system calibrates them separately for a
reason. A simulation starting at 10 AM should use morning_drift for steps
in hours 10-12 and afternoon_drift for steps in hours 12+.

This is analogous to how sigma_by_block and theta_am/theta_pm already switch
per-step. The drift should do the same.

Fix: In the MC simulation loop, select drift_adj based on the simulation
hour at each step, not the wall-clock hour at MC construction time:
  sim_et_hour = hour_offset + step / steps_per_hour
  if sim_et_hour < 12: drift = morning_drift
  else: drift = afternoon_drift

This is a small change but eliminates a systematic bias in morning simulations.


ISSUE B7: ou_max_stationary_std DEFAULT OF 2.0°F IS TOO LOOSE
----------------------------------------------------------------
Severity: MEDIUM-HIGH

The sigma cap formula:
  sigma_max = ou_max_stationary_std * sqrt(2 * theta)

With ou_max_stationary_std = 2.0 and theta_am = 0.2896:
  sigma_max = 2.0 * sqrt(0.579) = 2.0 * 0.761 = 1.52°F

This means paths can routinely wander 4°F (2 sigma of the stationary
distribution) from the attractor. On a day where the attractor is already
inflated by 5°F (Issue B1), this means paths can reach 9°F above the raw
NWP peak. That's physically absurd for a same-day temperature forecast.

The Phase 3 calibration (from NWP RMSE) is intended to fix this, but it
requires 10 dates and will produce a value of ~3.0°F (RMSE 2.0 * 1.5 factor),
which is EVEN LOOSER than the current 2.0. This is wrong — the RMSE * 1.5
formula is too generous.

The ou_max_stationary_std should represent how far the actual temperature
can deviate from the NWP forecast at any given hour, not the daily-high
prediction error. The hourly NWP error is smaller than the daily-high error
because the daily high is the maximum of 24 hourly errors.

Fix: Change the Phase 3 calibration to:
  ou_max_stationary_std = NWP_hourly_RMSE * 1.0 (no safety factor)
  NOT daily-high RMSE * 1.5.

For now, before Phase 3 activates, manually reduce the default from 2.0 to
1.5°F. This gives sigma_max = 1.5 * sqrt(0.579) = 1.14°F, which is tighter
but still allows the morning block sigma of 1.41 to be capped. This directly
reduces the width of the paths_max distribution and will eliminate some of the
phantom edge on extreme strikes.

PHASE A: CRITICAL FIXES
=========================
Priority: DO THIS IMMEDIATELY. These are causing wrong probabilities NOW.
Estimated effort: 1-2 days of Claude Code work.
Expected impact: Elimination of 100% fair value probabilities, reduction
  of attractor inflation from ~5°F to ~2°F.

ITEM A.1 — Remove drift_adj from the MC attractor
---------------------------------------------------
File changes: quant/monte_carlo.py, quant/mc_params_builder.py

Specification:

  A. In the MC simulation loop, change the attractor computation from:
       mu_t = nwp_curve[h] + nwp_anchor_offset + kalman_bias + drift_adj
     To:
       mu_t = nwp_curve[h] + nwp_anchor_offset + kalman_bias

  B. Do NOT delete drift calibration code. Keep calibrating drift_adj in
     calibration/calibrator.py and storing it in system_state. It should
     continue to be computed and logged as a DIAGNOSTIC metric.

  C. In the Streamlit UI (Tab 1 or Tab 4), display both kalman_bias and
     drift_adj side by side with a label like "Kalman bias (active)" and
     "Historical drift (diagnostic only)". This lets you monitor whether
     they're tracking the same signal.

  D. In MCParams, keep the drift fields but add a boolean flag
     use_drift_in_attractor (default False). This allows easy A/B testing
     via the backtester — run one set of replays with drift in the attractor
     and one without.

Rationale: With H=[[1,1]], the Kalman bias converges within hours and should
  absorb the same systematic error that drift_adj was compensating for. Adding
  both creates a ~3.2°F inflation (2.02 + 1.15) when the true correction is
  probably 1.5-2.5°F.

ITEM A.2 — Raise the persistence_filter_offset clamp
------------------------------------------------------
File changes: calibration/calibrator.py, config/settings.py

Specification:

  A. Change the clamp range from [0.0, 0.5] to [0.0, 1.5]°F.

  B. Re-run calibrate_persistence_offset() after the change. With 8 dates
     showing a mean gap of 0.75°F (including zeros) or 1.0°F (positive-only),
     the calibrated value should land around 0.75-1.0°F.

  C. Consider changing the calibration formula to use the MEAN of ALL gaps
     (including zeros), not just positive gaps. The zeros are informative —
     they represent days where the ASOS tabular max happened to land on a
     clean Celsius value that matched the true peak. Excluding them biases
     the offset upward.

  D. The current formula:
       offset = mean(gap for gap in gaps if gap > 0)
     Should become:
       offset = mean(gap for gap in gaps)  # include zeros
       offset = max(offset, 0.0)  # but never negative
     Then clamp to [0.0, 1.5].

Rationale: The empirical data strongly supports an offset of 0.75°F. The
  current 0.30°F is leaving ~0.45°F of systematic error uncorrected. This
  means the model assigns P(max >= hard_floor + 0.3) = 1.0 when it should
  assign P(max >= hard_floor + 0.75) = 1.0. For strikes near the hard floor,
  this matters.

ITEM A.3 — Reduce ou_max_stationary_std default and fix Phase 3 formula
--------------------------------------------------------------------------
File changes: config/settings.py, calibration/calibrator.py

Specification:

  A. Change the default ou_max_stationary_std from 2.0°F to 1.5°F.

  B. In calibrate_ou_max_stationary_std(), change the formula from:
       ou_max_stationary_std = blended_nwp_rmse * 1.5
     To:
       ou_max_stationary_std = blended_nwp_rmse * 1.0

     The 1.5x safety factor was too generous. The RMSE already represents
     the typical error; multiplying by 1.5 allows paths to wander 1.5x
     further than the NWP typically misses.

  C. Better yet: compute the HOURLY NWP RMSE rather than the daily-high RMSE.
     The ou_max_stationary_std constrains the per-hour stationary distribution,
     so it should be calibrated against per-hour errors, not daily-high errors.

     Specification for hourly RMSE:
       For each settled date:
         For each ET hour h where both ASOS reading and NWP curve are available:
           error_h = nearest_asos_temp(h) - blended_nwp_curve[h]
       hourly_rmse = sqrt(mean(error_h^2) across all (date, hour) pairs)
       ou_max_stationary_std = hourly_rmse * 1.2  (small safety factor)

     The hourly RMSE will be smaller than the daily-high RMSE (typically
     1.0-1.5°F vs 2.0°F) because it's the error at a single hour, not the
     maximum error across the day.

ITEM A.4 — Covariance Cap and Innovation Gate
-----------------------------------------------
File changes: quant/kalman_filter.py

Specification:

  A. After every update step (Kalman update), add:

     P_MAX_DIAG = 2.0
     # Cap diagonal elements
     P[0,0] = min(P[0,0], P_MAX_DIAG)
     P[1,1] = min(P[1,1], P_MAX_DIAG)
     # Enforce semi-definiteness (off-diagonal can't exceed geometric mean)
     max_offdiag = sqrt(P[0,0] * P[1,1]) * 0.8
     P[0,1] = np.clip(P[0,1], -max_offdiag, max_offdiag)
     P[1,0] = P[0,1]

  B. Also apply after warm-start initialization AND after gap inflation:
     After P = yesterday_P * 1.2 (warm-start), apply the cap.
     After each gap inflation predict step, apply the cap.
     This prevents P from starting the day in an explosive state.

  C. Before the update step, add an innovation gate:
     S = H @ P @ H.T + R
     innovation = z - H @ x
     mahalanobis = abs(innovation) / sqrt(S)
     if mahalanobis > 4.0:
         log.warning("Innovation gate fired", innovation=innovation,
                     mahalanobis=mahalanobis, asos_temp=z,
                     kalman_estimate=H @ x)
         return x, P  # skip update, return unchanged state

  D. Add a new setting: P_MAX_DIAGONAL (default 2.0) and
     INNOVATION_GATE_SIGMA (default 4.0) for tunability.
