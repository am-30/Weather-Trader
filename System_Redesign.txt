A. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
================================================================================

WHAT THE SYSTEM DOES WELL
--------------------------
1. Strike boundary semantics. The treatment of NWS integer rounding
   (X-0.5 / X+0.5 continuous thresholds for bucket boundaries) is correct
   and non-obvious. Many quant systems for these markets get this wrong.

2. Hard floor logic. Initializing paths_max at the observed daily max,
   not at T0 or negative infinity, is the single most important correctness
   property in the MC engine. The GREATEST() SQL pattern is also correct.

3. Bridge steps for post-rollover simulation. Recognizing that tonight's
   temperatures fall inside TODAY's NWS observation window and must not
   contaminate tomorrow's paths_max shows strong domain understanding.

4. Joseph-form Kalman update. Using the numerically stable form prevents
   the covariance matrix from losing positive definiteness over long runs.
   Most hobby implementations skip this and then wonder why their filter
   diverges after 48 hours.

5. The 4-source ASOS fallback chain with dynamic limit scaling. The
   N = min(500, max(10, gap_minutes * 3)) fix for overnight gaps shows
   good operational awareness.

6. NWP detrending in sigma calibration. Subtracting the NWP-predicted
   hourly change before computing sigma is essential. Without it, the
   diurnal warming/cooling trend inflates sigma 2-3x. Many implementations
   miss this entirely.

7. Warm-start with 20% covariance inflation. Carrying yesterday's bias
   estimate forward while inflating P by 1.2x is a reasonable heuristic
   for the bias-persistence / uncertainty-growth tradeoff overnight.


WHAT'S MISSING (The Hard Truth)
---------------------------------
The system has strong engineering and solid domain knowledge of the ASOS/NWS
pipeline, but it has a fundamental gap that would concern any professional
quant: there is no way to measure whether the model has edge. Without a
backtesting framework that replays historical days through the exact same
pipeline and compares model probabilities to outcomes, every calibration
choice and every parameter is an untested hypothesis.

Additionally, the OU simulation model has several structural simplifications
that introduce systematic biases in the probability estimates. These are not
bugs — they are modeling choices that happen to be wrong in predictable
directions, and they can be fixed without changing the overall architecture.

The remainder of this document identifies each issue, explains WHY it matters
for P&L, and provides specifications for fixing it in priority order.

B. CRITICAL CONCEPTUAL ISSUES
================================================================================

These are not code bugs. They are places where the quantitative reasoning
needs sharpening. Understanding them conceptually is as important as fixing
the code, because similar reasoning errors will recur in future extensions.


ISSUE B1: SIGMA IS NOT CONSTANT ACROSS THE DAY
------------------------------------------------
Problem:
  The system calibrates a single sigma (°F/sqrt-hr) from pooled hourly
  differences across all 24 hours. But temperature volatility has strong
  intraday structure:

  - Pre-dawn (4-6 AM ET): Very low volatility. Temperature is near its
    diurnal minimum, radiative cooling is slow, wind is calm. Sigma ~0.2-0.3.
  - Morning ramp (8-11 AM): High volatility. Solar heating competes with
    residual cool air, cloud breaks cause sharp jumps. Sigma ~0.6-1.0.
  - Afternoon peak (1-4 PM): Moderate volatility. Temperature is near its
    maximum, convective mixing dominates. Sigma ~0.4-0.7.
  - Evening decline (5-8 PM): Low-moderate volatility. Cooling is systematic.
    Sigma ~0.3-0.5.

  These ranges are illustrative for KBOS in spring. The point is that they
  differ by 2-3x across the day.

Why it matters for P&L:
  A pooled sigma of 0.6 is too HIGH in the afternoon (when paths have only
  a few hours left to run and the NWP error is small) and too LOW in the
  morning (when the day's outcome is genuinely uncertain). This creates a
  systematic pattern:
    - Morning: model UNDERESTIMATES tail probabilities → misses edge on
      far-from-consensus strikes.
    - Afternoon: model OVERESTIMATES tail probabilities → sees phantom edge
      on strikes that are actually dead.

Fix specification (Phase 1, Item 1):
  Calibrate sigma as a function of ET hour. Partition the day into 4-6
  blocks (e.g. 0-6, 6-10, 10-14, 14-18, 18-24 ET) and compute a separate
  sigma for each block using the existing hourly-difference method.

  Store as a JSON array in system_state (e.g. sigma_by_block: [0.25, 0.7,
  0.55, 0.4, 0.3]). In the MC simulation loop, look up sigma_used from the
  block corresponding to the current simulation hour (hour_offset + step//12).

  The OU sigma cap (ou_max_stationary_std) should also be applied per-block,
  since the stationary distribution is block-dependent.


ISSUE B2: THETA IS NOT CONSTANT ACROSS THE DAY EITHER
------------------------------------------------------
Problem:
  Mean-reversion speed varies with the dominant physical process:
  - During solar heating (morning): theta is LOW because the atmosphere is
    actively being forced — departures from NWP persist because both the
    NWP and the real atmosphere are responding to the same solar forcing,
    but with different clouds, albedo, etc.
  - Near peak / post-peak: theta is HIGH because convective mixing acts as
    a strong thermostat — warm parcels rise, cool air descends, and
    temperature is pulled back toward the large-scale prediction.
  - At night: theta is MODERATE. Radiative cooling is systematic but slow.

Why it matters:
  A single theta=0.3 means half-life ~2.3 hours everywhere. In the morning,
  this pulls paths back to the NWP attractor too aggressively, suppressing
  legitimate departures. In the afternoon, it's about right. The net effect
  is similar to B1: morning probabilities are too narrow, afternoon
  probabilities are slightly too wide.

Fix specification (Phase 2, Item 1):
  This is harder to calibrate than sigma because theta requires AR(1) fits
  within each block, and 1-2 weeks of data won't give stable estimates for
  6 blocks. Recommendation: defer this to Phase 2. For now, use a simple
  two-regime theta: theta_am (hours 6-13) and theta_pm (hours 13-20),
  calibrated from the existing AR(1) approach but split into two pools.
  Start with the 60-day backfill data.


ISSUE B3: THE OU PROCESS CANNOT REPRESENT FRONTAL PASSAGES
------------------------------------------------------------
Problem:
  The OU process assumes temperature fluctuates around a smooth NWP curve
  with Gaussian noise. On days with frontal passages, the temperature
  distribution is BIMODAL: either the front arrives before peak (cooler
  outcome) or after peak (warmer outcome). The timing uncertainty is the
  dominant source of outcome uncertainty, not Gaussian diffusion around
  the NWP forecast.

  This matters most in spring and fall at KBOS, where frontal passages
  are common and can cause 10-15°F swings in 2-3 hours.

Why it matters for P&L:
  On frontal days, the OU model produces a unimodal distribution centered
  near the NWP forecast. The true distribution is bimodal. This means:
  - Strikes near the NWP forecast are OVERPRICED by the model (the model
    assigns high probability to the mode, but the true probability mass is
    split between two outcomes, each of which has lower probability at the
    NWP forecast value).
  - Strikes at the extremes (very warm or very cool) may be UNDERPRICED
    because the bimodal tails extend further than a Gaussian.

  However: this is hard to model correctly and the market may also be wrong
  on these days. This is a Phase 3 item — awareness is more important than
  a fix right now.

Fix specification (Phase 3, Item 1):
  Use NWP ensemble spread as a regime detector. Open-Meteo provides ensemble
  forecasts for GFS (31 members) and ECMWF (51 members) for free. When the
  ensemble spread for the daily max is large (>4°F), the atmospheric state
  is uncertain — likely a frontal day. In these cases:
  - Increase sigma by a multiplicative factor (e.g. 1.5x).
  - Or: run two OU simulations (one with a "front arrives early" NWP curve,
    one with "front arrives late") and mix the resulting CDFs.
  The simpler approach (inflate sigma) is recommended first.


ISSUE B4: THE NWP ANCHOR OFFSET IS AD HOC
-------------------------------------------
Problem:
  The anchor_weight formula (1 - hours_to_peak/peak_hour_idx) is a linear
  interpolation with no physical or statistical motivation. It's trying to
  answer: "how much of the current T0-vs-NWP gap should I project onto
  the peak?" This is a legitimate question, but the linear answer is wrong
  in both tails:

  - At 6 AM (hours_to_peak = 8, peak_idx = 8): weight = 0. This says
    "ignore the current gap entirely." But if T0 is 5°F below NWP at 6 AM,
    some of that gap IS predictive of the peak — it suggests the air mass
    is genuinely cooler than the NWP assumed.

  - At 12 PM (hours_to_peak = 2, peak_idx = 8): weight = 0.75. This says
    "project 75% of the gap." But by noon, the Kalman bias estimate B_t
    should already be absorbing most of the systematic error. The anchor
    offset is double-counting the Kalman correction.

Why it matters:
  Double-counting the Kalman bias and the anchor offset is a systematic
  error. If B_t = -1.5°F (NWP runs warm) and the anchor offset is also
  pulling mu_t down by 1.0°F, the effective correction is -2.5°F — too
  aggressive. This depresses the probability distribution and creates
  phantom edge on UNDER contracts.

Fix specification (Phase 1, Item 2):
  Replace the anchor offset with a simpler, more defensible approach:

  Option A (recommended): Remove the anchor offset entirely. The Kalman
  bias B_t already serves this purpose. The mu_t formula becomes:
    mu_t = nwp_curve[hour_idx] + bias + drift_adj
  Monitor the first 10 days of backtesting to see if this degrades
  accuracy. If it does, add back a SMALL, constant offset equal to
  (T0 - nwp_curve[hour_offset] - bias) * 0.3, with no time-varying weight.

  Option B: Keep the anchor offset but subtract the Kalman bias from it:
    raw_gap = T0 - nwp_curve[hour_offset]
    gap_after_bias = raw_gap - bias
    nwp_anchor_offset = gap_after_bias * anchor_weight
  This prevents double-counting. The anchor offset now only captures the
  RESIDUAL gap after Kalman correction.

  Option B is more conservative (smaller change to existing behavior).
  Implement Option B first, then A/B test against the current approach in
  the backtester.


ISSUE B5: CALIBRATION LOOKBACK IS TOO SHORT AND UNWEIGHTED
------------------------------------------------------------
Problem:
  Sigma and theta are calibrated on a 7-day rolling window. With hourly
  data, 7 days gives ~168 hourly differences — barely adequate for a single
  sigma estimate, and far too few for per-block sigma (28 samples per block
  for a 6-block partition).

  Additionally, the window is unweighted: a reading from 7 days ago counts
  the same as one from yesterday. In weather, recent conditions (air mass,
  season, synoptic pattern) are more predictive of today's volatility than
  conditions a week ago.

Why it matters:
  Noisy calibration → noisy probabilities → noisy edges → noisy trades.
  With 7-day calibration, a single anomalous day (thunderstorm, instrument
  error) can shift sigma by 30-50%, creating phantom edge for the next week.

Fix specification (Phase 2, Item 2):
  1. Extend the default lookback to 30 days (not 60 — too much seasonal
     drift for spring at KBOS where March and May are very different).
  2. Apply exponential decay weighting: weight(day_d) = exp(-d / tau)
     where tau = 10 days (so a reading from 10 days ago has weight 0.37,
     from 20 days ago has weight 0.14). Normalize weights to sum to 1.
  3. For sigma_by_block, use the full 30-day window to get adequate samples
     per block (~120 per 5-hour block). Still apply exponential weighting.
  4. Theta calibration: same 30-day window with exponential weighting.
  5. Model weights (Brier scoring): extend to 14-day window minimum.
     With only 2-7 days of Kalshi strike data, the Brier scores are pure
     noise. Until you have 14+ settled days with known strikes, use fixed
     equal weights (1/3 each) or a simple RMSE-based weighting on the NWP
     predicted daily high vs actual daily high (no MC needed for this).


ISSUE B6: NO MODEL FOR THE NWS ROUNDING / ASOS PERSISTENCE FILTER GAP
------------------------------------------------------------------------
Problem:
  Section 1 of the architecture doc correctly identifies that the NWS daily
  max can exceed the highest ASOS tabular reading by 0.2-0.4°F due to the
  0.5°C persistence filter. But the system doesn't model this gap.

  The hard floor is set from ASOS readings (max of temperature_f and
  max6h_f). The MC paths evolve from T0 (a Kalman estimate based on ASOS
  data). Neither incorporates the fact that the TRUE temperature is
  systematically slightly higher than what ASOS reports.

Why it matters:
  When a strike boundary falls within the 0.4°F gap between the highest
  ASOS reading and the true NWS daily max, the model assigns ~0% to the
  higher outcome when the true probability is 10-30%. This is a free edge
  to anyone who understands the persistence filter.

  Example: ASOS max reading = 39.1°F (which is 3.94°C, just below the
  4.0°C = 39.2°F sensor threshold). The true temperature may have briefly
  hit 39.4°F (rounds to 39°F for NWS) or even 39.6°F (rounds to 40°F).
  A B40 strike priced at 3 cents might be worth 15 cents.

Fix specification (Phase 1, Item 3):
  Add a small positive bias to the hard floor to account for the persistence
  filter gap. Specifically:

  1. Define a parameter PERSISTENCE_FILTER_OFFSET (default 0.3°F). This
     represents the expected gap between ASOS tabular max and true max.
  2. In the MC simulation, set:
       effective_hard_floor = hard_floor + PERSISTENCE_FILTER_OFFSET
     Use this ONLY for paths_max initialization, not for the hard floor
     stored in the database (which should remain the conservative ASOS value).
  3. Better yet: calibrate this offset from historical data. For each
     settled day, compute: gap = final_official_high_raw - max(ASOS readings).
     (Use the raw NWS value before integer rounding, if available; otherwise
     use the integer value.) The mean gap across 30+ days is your calibrated
     PERSISTENCE_FILTER_OFFSET. Store it in system_state.

  Note: The max6h_f field partially captures this (it's the 6-hour maximum
  from the METAR 1-group), but it's also subject to METAR rounding artifacts
  (reported in whole °C, converted to °F). The offset approach is more robust.

ITEM 0.1 — Historical Replay Engine
-------------------------------------
Purpose: Given a historical date and a time-of-day, reconstruct the exact
MCParams that would have been used at that moment, run the MC simulation,
and compare the resulting probabilities to the actual NWS outcome.

File: backtesting/replay_engine.py

Specification:

  class ReplayEngine:
      def replay_date(self, target_date: date, eval_hours: list[int]) -> list[ReplayResult]:
          """
          For each hour in eval_hours (ET hours, e.g. [8, 10, 12, 14, 16]):
          1. Query asos_readings for target_date WHERE observation_time_utc <= hour.
             Use the latest reading as the "current" ASOS temp.
          2. Compute hard_floor = max(temperature_f, max6h_f) across all readings
             up to that hour.
          3. Query nwp_forecasts for target_date. Use the latest fetch BEFORE eval_hour
             for each model. Blend using the model_weights that were active on that date
             (from system_state) or equal weights if not available.
          4. Query system_state for target_date. Use kalman_bias_estimate and
             kalman_covariance. If not available, use bias=0, identity covariance.
          5. Call build_mc_params_historical(target_date, hour, state, asos_temp,
             hard_floor, blended_curve).
          6. Run price_full_distribution(mc_params, strike_list).
          7. Get actual outcome: final_official_high from markets table.
          8. Return ReplayResult with:
             - target_date, eval_hour
             - mc_params used (for debugging)
             - predicted probabilities per strike
             - actual_high
             - For each strike: predicted P(YES), actual outcome (1 or 0),
               squared error (Brier component)
          """

  @dataclass
  class ReplayResult:
      target_date: date
      eval_hour: int
      T0: float
      hard_floor: float
      sigma: float
      theta: float
      bias: float
      nwp_predicted_high: float
      actual_high: float
      strike_probs: dict[float, float]      # strike -> P(max >= strike)
      market_probs: dict[str, float]        # ticker -> P(YES)
      brier_scores: dict[str, float]        # ticker -> (p - outcome)^2
      mean_max: float
      std_max: float

  def replay_all(self, start_date: date, end_date: date,
                  eval_hours: list[int] = [8, 10, 12, 14, 16]) -> pd.DataFrame:
      """Run replay_date for every settled date in range. Return a DataFrame
      with one row per (date, hour) pair. This is the primary analysis artifact."""

Key constraints for the implementor:
  - Use build_mc_params_historical, NOT build_mc_params. The live builder
    uses datetime.now() internally; the historical builder takes explicit time.
  - The strike list should come from the actual Kalshi markets on that date.
    Query the markets table for target_date to get floor_strike / cap_strike.
    If no market data is stored for that date, use a default range centered
    on the NWP predicted high (e.g. predicted_high ± 8°F in 1°F increments).
  - Store replay results in a new table (replay_results) for UI display,
    OR return them as a DataFrame for notebook analysis. Both is ideal.
  - Use the SAME random seed (np.random.default_rng(42)) for all replays
    so results are reproducible. Add a seed parameter for sensitivity testing.

ITEM 0.2 — Aggregate Accuracy Metrics
---------------------------------------
Purpose: Compute summary statistics that tell you whether the model is
calibrated, biased, or overconfident.

File: backtesting/metrics.py

Specification:

  def compute_backtest_metrics(results: pd.DataFrame) -> dict:
      """
      Input: DataFrame from replay_all().
      Output dict with:

      1. Brier Score (overall):
         mean of (predicted_prob - outcome)^2 across all (date, hour, strike) triples.
         Lower is better. A climatological baseline (always predict the historical
         base rate) gives the "no skill" Brier score for comparison.

      2. Calibration curve data:
         Bin predicted probabilities into deciles (0-10%, 10-20%, ..., 90-100%).
         For each bin: mean predicted probability, observed frequency of outcome=1.
         Perfect calibration: these two values are equal. Overconfidence: predicted
         probabilities are more extreme than observed frequencies.

      3. Mean bias:
         mean(mean_max - actual_high) across all (date, hour) pairs.
         Positive = model systematically overestimates temperature.

      4. RMSE of mean_max vs actual_high:
         sqrt(mean((mean_max - actual_high)^2)).
         Compare to NWP RMSE (the "dumb baseline").

      5. Log-loss:
         -mean(outcome * log(p) + (1-outcome) * log(1-p)) per strike.
         More sensitive to confident wrong predictions than Brier score.

      6. Per-hour breakdown of all metrics above:
         Critical for diagnosing time-of-day biases (Issue B1, B2).

      7. Sharpness:
         mean(|predicted_prob - 0.5|) across all predictions.
         Higher = more decisive predictions. Meaningless without calibration,
         but useful paired with calibration: sharp AND calibrated = good model.
      """

  def compute_climatological_baseline(results: pd.DataFrame,
                                       historical_highs: list[float]) -> dict:
      """
      The "no model" baseline. For each strike, predict the historical
      frequency that the daily max exceeded that strike. This is the bar
      your model must clear to have any edge at all.

      historical_highs: list of NWS daily max for KBOS for the past 5-10
      years for the same calendar month(s). Source: IEM Mesonet CLImate data
      or NOAA GHCN-Daily (both free).

      Returns same metrics dict as compute_backtest_metrics for comparison.
      """

Key constraints for the implementor:
  - Brier score must be computed per-strike and aggregated, not on the
    mean_max directly. The model is making probability statements about
    specific strike thresholds, not point forecasts.
  - The calibration curve is the single most important diagnostic. If
    events predicted at 80% happen only 60% of the time, the model is
    overconfident — you will systematically overbuy. Plot it.
  - Include 90% confidence intervals on all metrics using bootstrap
    resampling (resample dates with replacement, 1000 iterations).
    With only 10-14 days of data, point estimates are meaningless
    without uncertainty bands.

ITEM 0.3 — Backtest Comparison Framework
------------------------------------------
Purpose: Allow A/B comparison of model variants. When you change sigma
calibration or remove the anchor offset, you need to measure whether it
actually improved accuracy or just felt right.

File: backtesting/comparison.py

Specification:

  def compare_variants(baseline_results: pd.DataFrame,
                        variant_results: pd.DataFrame,
                        label_baseline: str = "current",
                        label_variant: str = "proposed") -> dict:
      """
      Compute paired differences in Brier score, RMSE, calibration error.
      Use a paired bootstrap test: for each of 1000 bootstrap resamples
      (sampling dates with replacement), compute the difference in Brier
      score. Report:
        - Mean difference (positive = variant is worse)
        - 95% CI on the difference
        - P-value (fraction of bootstrap samples where variant is worse)
      If the 95% CI includes zero, you don't have enough data to conclude
      the variant is better. DO NOT deploy it just because the point
      estimate looks good.
      """

Key constraints for the implementor:
  - The unit of resampling is the DATE, not individual (date, hour, strike)
    triples. Predictions within a single day are correlated (same weather),
    so resampling individual predictions inflates significance.
  - Print a clear summary like:
    "Variant 'time_varying_sigma' Brier: 0.082 vs baseline 0.095.
     Improvement: 0.013 (95% CI: [-0.004, 0.031]). NOT significant at p=0.05."

ITEM 1.1 — Time-Varying Sigma
-------------------------------
(See Issue B1 for full rationale.)

File changes: calibration/calibrator.py, quant/monte_carlo.py, db/schemas.py

Specification:

  A. Define hour blocks:
     SIGMA_BLOCKS = [(0, 6), (6, 10), (10, 14), (14, 18), (18, 24)]

  B. In estimate_sigma_from_historical():
     - After computing all hourly dT_residual values, ALSO record which
       ET hour each dT_residual came from.
     - For each block, compute sigma_block = sqrt(mean(dT_residual^2))
       for all dT_residuals where the source hour falls in that block.
     - Clamp each sigma_block to [0.05, 2.0].
     - Return both the pooled sigma (for backward compatibility) and
       a sigma_by_block dict: {block_label: sigma_value}.
     - Fall back to pooled sigma for any block with fewer than 10 samples.

  C. In system_state table:
     - Add column sigma_by_block JSONB (nullable).
     - Store the dict from step B.

  D. In MCParams:
     - Add field sigma_by_block: dict[str, float] | None.
     - build_mc_params populates from system_state. Falls back to
       {all_blocks: sigma} if sigma_by_block is None.

  E. In the MC simulation loop (run_simulation or price_full_distribution):
     - At each step, determine the current ET hour:
         sim_hour = hour_offset + step / steps_per_hour
     - Look up which block sim_hour falls in.
     - Use the corresponding sigma_block (after applying the OU sigma cap
       per-block: sigma_used = min(sigma_block, max_std * sqrt(2*theta))).
     - The per-step noise becomes: sigma_block * sqrt(dt) * Z[step].

  F. In the OU sigma cap logic:
     - Apply the cap independently to each block's sigma. A high-volatility
       morning block should be capped to a higher value than a low-volatility
       nighttime block? No — the cap is about the STATIONARY distribution
       relative to the NWP attractor, which is the same physical constraint
       regardless of time-of-day. Keep the same cap. But compute
       sigma_max = ou_max_stationary_std * sqrt(2 * theta) once, and
       apply it to each block independently.

ITEM 1.2 — Fix the NWP Anchor Offset Double-Counting
------------------------------------------------------
(See Issue B4 for full rationale.)

File changes: quant/monte_carlo.py

Specification (Option B — conservative fix):

  In the code that computes nwp_anchor_offset, replace:
    nwp_anchor_offset = (T0 - nwp_curve[hour_offset]) * anchor_weight

  With:
    raw_gap = T0 - nwp_curve[hour_offset]
    gap_after_bias = raw_gap - bias   # bias = Kalman B_t
    nwp_anchor_offset = gap_after_bias * anchor_weight

  This ensures the anchor offset only captures the RESIDUAL gap after the
  Kalman bias correction, preventing double-counting.

  After implementing, run the backtester (Phase 0) and compare Brier scores
  against the current approach. If the improvement is not statistically
  significant, consider Option A (remove anchor offset entirely) and test.

ITEM 1.3 — Persistence Filter Offset
--------------------------------------
(See Issue B6 for full rationale.)

File changes: quant/monte_carlo.py, calibration/calibrator.py, config/settings.py

Specification:

  A. Add setting: PERSISTENCE_FILTER_OFFSET = 0.3 (°F, default).

  B. In calibrator, add function calibrate_persistence_offset():
     - For each settled date in the past 30 days:
         max_asos = max(temperature_f, max6h_f) across all readings for date.
         official = final_official_high from markets table.
         gap = official - max_asos
     - offset = mean(gap) across all dates where gap > 0.
       (Negative gaps mean ASOS overread, which is rare but possible with
       the max6h_f field; exclude these.)
     - Clamp to [0.0, 0.5].
     - Store in system_state or return for MCParams.

  C. In the MC simulation:
     - When initializing paths_max:
         effective_hard_floor = hard_floor + persistence_filter_offset
         paths_max = np.full(n_paths, effective_hard_floor)
     - Do NOT modify the hard floor in the database. This offset is
       a model correction, not a data correction.

  D. In build_mc_params: add persistence_filter_offset field. Populate from
     calibration or default.

ITEM 3.2 — Cloud Cover as a Regime Indicator
----------------------------------------------
Open-Meteo provides cloudcover (%) in the same API call as temperature.
File changes: ingestion/nwp_fetcher.py, quant/monte_carlo.py

Specification:
  A. Add &hourly=cloudcover to the existing NWP API calls.
  B. Compute mean cloudcover for hours 10-16 ET (the solar heating window).
  C. On heavily overcast days (mean cloudcover > 80%), the NWP is usually
     more accurate (cloud-dominated days have less convective variability).
     Reduce sigma_by_block by a factor of 0.8.
  D. On clear days (mean cloudcover < 20%), convective variability is higher.
     Increase sigma_by_block by a factor of 1.1.
  E. Validate via backtesting. This is hypothesis-driven: the prediction is
     that clear days have higher sigma and overcast days have lower sigma.
     If the backtest doesn't confirm this, remove the adjustment.

ITEM 3.3 — Historical Climatology Baseline
--------------------------------------------

PHASE 4: EDGE VALIDATION FRAMEWORK
====================================
Priority: HIGH (but depends on accumulating 30+ days of data).
Estimated effort: 1 day of Claude Code work.

ITEM 4.1 — Edge Source Attribution
------------------------------------
Purpose: Decompose your model's edge (or lack thereof) into components.

File: backtesting/edge_analysis.py

Specification:

  For each settled date, compute the Brier score under several model variants:
  1. Climatology only (no model, no NWP).
  2. NWP point forecast only (P(YES) = 1 if predicted_high > strike, else 0).
  3. NWP + sigma (OU simulation with flat sigma, no Kalman, no drift).
  4. Full model (all corrections: Kalman, drift, time-varying sigma, etc.).

  The DIFFERENCE in Brier score between successive variants is the marginal
  value of each component:
    Edge from NWP vs climatology: Brier(1) - Brier(2)
    Edge from uncertainty modeling: Brier(2) - Brier(3)
    Edge from Kalman + corrections: Brier(3) - Brier(4)

  If the edge from Kalman + corrections is negative (makes things worse),
  those components are hurting you and should be simplified.

  Present as a waterfall chart in the Streamlit UI.

ITEM 4.2 — Simulated P&L Under Historical Prices
---------------------------------------------------
Purpose: Even if your model is well-calibrated, you only make money if your
edge exceeds the bid-ask spread AND you can get filled at the prices you see.

Specification:
  - For each settled date where you have Kalshi market prices stored, compute
    the trade the system would have made (using the stored edge and Kelly sizing).
  - Compute P&L: if outcome=YES and you bought YES at ask price p, profit = (1-p).
    If outcome=NO, loss = -p. Scale by contracts.
  - Track cumulative P&L, max drawdown, Sharpe ratio (annualized).
  - Compare to: always betting the climatological favorite, always passing.
  - WARNING: This is NOT a true backtest because you don't have historical
    order book data (fill rates, slippage). It's a ceiling on performance.
    Real P&L will be worse.

D. ADDITIONAL RECOMMENDATIONS (NOT PHASED — ONGOING)
================================================================================

D1. LOGGING FOR POST-MORTEM ANALYSIS
  Every MC run should log (to a new mc_run_logs table or JSONB in snapshots):
  - The exact MCParams used (serialized).
  - Mean, std, p10, p25, p50, p75, p90 of paths_max.
  - Wall-clock time of the MC run.
  - The full strike probability dict.
  This is critical for debugging days where the model was wrong. Without it,
  you'll be guessing which parameters were active when a bad prediction was made.

D2. MONITOR THE ENSEMBLE-DETERMINISTIC GAP
  When you have ensemble data (Phase 3), track:
    det_high = deterministic NWP predicted high
    ens_mean_high = mean of ensemble member predicted highs
  If these differ by more than 1°F, the deterministic model is an outlier
  relative to its own ensemble — treat with caution.

D3. CONSIDER REDUCING MC PATHS DURING BACKTESTING
  10,000 paths per run × 5 eval hours × 30 dates = 1.5 million MC runs.
  For backtesting, 2,000 paths is adequate (variance of Brier score from
  MC noise << variance from model error). Parameterize n_paths so backtesting
  runs 5x faster.

D4. WATCH FOR ASOS OUTAGES AROUND PEAK HOURS
  If no ASOS reading is received between 12-4 PM ET, the hard floor may be
  stale. Add a diagnostic flag (peak_hours_coverage) that counts the number
  of ASOS readings in the 12-4 PM window. If < 2, flag the day as
  "low confidence hard floor" in the UI and in mc_run_logs.

D5. THE MARKET IS PROBABLY EFFICIENT ON EASY DAYS
  On days where the NWP models agree closely (ensemble spread < 2°F) and
  the forecast is far from any strike boundary (e.g. predicted high = 45°F,
  nearest strike at 42°F), the market will be correctly priced by even
  simple models. Your edge, if any, comes from:
  1. Hard days (frontal passages, marginal strikes).
  2. The persistence filter gap (Issue B6) — this is structural and
     unlikely to be priced by most market participants.
  3. Late-day information (after 2 PM, you have most of the day's data
     and the market may not update fast enough).
  Focus your analysis on these scenarios specifically.
